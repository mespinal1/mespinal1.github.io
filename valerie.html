What is a climate refugee?

A climate refugee is a person who has been forced to leave their home as a result of the effects of climate change on their environment.

However, the term "climate refugee" does not exist in international law; neither the United Nations of any international agency that works with refugees or climate change recognises any particular definition or name. Thus, climate refugees may also be referred to as climate migrants or environmental migrants. Although the term "climate refugee" is popularly used in media, it can be misleading for the following reasons:

  • As of now, climate refugees do not/will not have the same legal status as other refugees acknowledged by international law.
  • Most climate refugees will be internally displaced. This means that rather than fleeing international borders, as many traditional refugees would, climate refugees would migrate to other areas within their country of residence.
  • Many people fleeing climate disasters would dislike being referred to as a "refugee", as this term comes with many stigmas and legal and social assumptions.

How do we expect climate change to create climate refugees?

Currently, extremely hot, unlivable zones like the Sahara, cover less than 1% of Earth. By 2070, unlivable hot zones could cover nearly 1/5 of the land, placing 1 of every 3 people in an unlivable zone.

Climate change is forcing families to flee water scarcity, crop failure, and rising sea levels, as well as worsening natural disasters. The effects of climate change will affect the poorest people the worst, especially in developing countries; in Sub-Saharan Afica, South Asia, and Latin America, it is estimated that as many as 143 million people could be displaced by climate change by 2050. Such a shift will overburden already crowded urban areas, especially in developing countries that already are behind when it comes to infrastructure, housing, and public services.

Even if urgent steps are taken to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and governments commit to combating climate change, in a best case scenario, 40 million people from those regions would still be displaced.

Northern nations will naturally take on many climate migrants:

  • The natural environments of countries in the global north are less susceptible in many ways to the effects of climate change, as opposed to those in the global south or those near the equator
  • Northern nations tend to be more developed and have better economies than southern ones
  • Many nations in the global North face a demographic decline that could in fact benefit from younger, fit-to-work incoming migrants

    • However, many Northern nations, notably the United States, have strong opposition to immigration, especially the large influx of immigrats that is expected to arise from climate change. A proper acceptance of climate migrants would first require the choice of a country to allow and welcome migrants, rather than continue to shut them out. It will also require good will, careful management of political differences, and the preparation of climate refuge cities.

    How can we prepare for climate migrants?

    The future of sustainable urban development

    A key way in which we can prepare for climate migrants is through sustainable urban development - developing cities in ways that are environmentally sustainable, culturally diverse and welcoming, and full of economic opportunities. Through sustainable urban development, cities will not only be prepared for, but be able to benefit from, incoming climate migrants. Although sustainable urban development is complex and can differ depending on each city's unique situation, there are many sustainable initiatives that are generally viewed as extremely important and beneficial to any urban area:

  • Protecting green space and the natural environment of urban areas
  • Pedestrianizing cities by banning cars in zones within the city and designating more streets to people and bikes
  • Redeveloping vacant lots and buildings into housing, parks, small solar farms, gardens, or other local initiatives
  • Utilizing compact design by developing up or under, rather than out
  • Diversifying housing options, providing a place to live for people of diverse incomes, lifestyles, and demographics
  • Providing high-quality public transportation
  • Adopt people-first policies that prioritize cultural diversity, education, healthcare, and the overall wellbeing of people living in cities, especially those from underprivileged communities

    • Potential climate refuge cities

    It is crucial that future climate refuge cities have:

    1. relatively low-risk of being affected by climate-related disasters such as flooding, wildfires, storms, extreme heat, and drought
    2. sustainable access to water

    Some cities may be better equipped than others to receive climate refugees. For example, cities in the US Rust Belt have historical advantages. They used to be major manufacturing centers, but due to the globalization of steel, automobile, and other manufacturing industries, by the 21st century, there are many vacant properties of low value. This makes Rust Belt cities good candidates for climate-centered redevelopment projects. Additionally, Rust Belt cities are not threatened by rising sea levels but still have access to water.

    Cities that have been identified by experts as ideal receiver cities include Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, Portland, Oregon, and Charlotte, North Carolina.
